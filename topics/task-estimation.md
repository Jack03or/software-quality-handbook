# Task Estimation in Scrum

## Overview
Task estimation in Scrum helps the team discuss work before it starts and gives a rough idea of the effort involved.

## Why It Matters
- Helps surface hidden risks early, such as edge cases, unclear requirements, or technical debt before development begins  
- Builds shared understanding within the team by encouraging discussion and identifying assumptions  
- Improves planning accuracy over time as teams learn from previous estimates and completed work  
- Reduces pressure by treating estimates as rough forecasts rather than fixed commitments  
- Makes large or unclear tasks more visible, encouraging teams to break them down before starting development  

## Best Practices
- Estimate as a team to get multiple perspectives and avoid relying on one person  
- Break large or unclear tasks into smaller ones before estimating  
- Use relative estimation instead of exact hours to improve consistency  
- Review estimates based on previous sprint experience and adjust over time  
- Use past work or reference stories to guide new estimates  
- Discuss assumptions during estimation so hidden risks are identified early  
- Consider dependencies such as other teams, APIs, or reviews  
- Be realistic about uncertainty instead of giving overly optimistic estimates  
- Keep estimation sessions focused and avoid over-discussing small differences  

## Bad Practices to Avoid
- Letting one person estimate everything instead of involving the team  
- Treating estimates as fixed promises rather than rough forecasts  
- Estimating tasks that are too large or unclear  
- Ignoring uncertainty, edge cases, or hidden work  
- Making optimistic guesses due to pressure from deadlines or management  
- Forgetting non-coding work such as testing, bug fixing, or code reviews  
- Treating story points like hours instead of relative effort  
- Not using past experience or velocity to guide estimates  
- Ignoring dependencies or external factors during planning  
- Failing to discuss assumptions, leading to misunderstandings  

## Common Challenges
- Estimation often becomes inaccurate due to hidden complexity. Tasks that seem simple can involve edge cases, unclear requirements, or technical debt that only becomes visible during development.
- Another common issue is forgetting non-coding work such as testing, code reviews, and deployment delays. These are often not considered during initial estimation, leading to underestimation.
- Different team members may also have different assumptions about the scope of a task. If these assumptions are not discussed, it can result in inconsistent estimates and misunderstandings.
- Dependencies are a major challenge in estimation. Tasks that rely on other teams, external services, or third-party APIs can take longer than expected, even if the coding work itself is small.
- Teams can also struggle when they lack historical data or experience. New teams often find estimation difficult because they do not yet have a clear baseline for comparison.
- Another issue is pressure to give quick or precise estimates. This can lead to overly optimistic guesses rather than realistic forecasts.
- Finally, estimation can be affected by team dynamics. If one person dominates discussions, it can reduce the accuracy of the estimate and limit input from others.

## Example
During sprint planning, a team estimates a simple UI feature at 3 story points based on similar past work. However, once development starts, they discover edge cases in form validation, browser compatibility issues, and additional testing requirements that were not visible at the start. The task ends up taking significantly more effort than expected.

This shows how hidden complexity and overlooked work can cause estimates to be inaccurate, even when the team has prior experience with similar tasks.

## Notes from Reading Sources
**Why Estimating Tasks is So Hard (Adrian Lisboa):**
- Tasks often take longer due to hidden issues like unclear requirements, edge cases, or technical debt  
- Important work like testing and bug fixing is often missed during estimation  
- Pressure to give quick estimates leads to unrealistic guesses  

**The Art and Struggle of Software Estimations (Socrates Meetup):**
- Different team members have different assumptions, so estimation works better as a group  
- Estimates are unreliable when tasks are not clearly broken down  
- Dependencies on other teams or systems can cause delays  

**Agile Alliance (Story Points / Lessons Learnt):**
- Estimation improves over time as teams gain experience  
- Breaking tasks down leads to better accuracy  
- Relative estimation is more effective than estimating exact time  

**Mountain Goat Software:**
- Estimation should be done as a team using shared reference points  
- Story points should not be converted into hours  
- Estimates should be treated as forecasts, not commitments
